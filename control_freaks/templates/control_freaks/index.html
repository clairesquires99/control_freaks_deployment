about this blog

Control Freaks is a space inspired by my own (fairly chaotic) experiences on contraception, as well as the varied experiences of loved ones, friends and complete strangers. For me, experiencing emotional lows and being told by my doctor that it couldn’t possibly be my contraception that was causing it, resulted in me staying on it for years. The moment I stopped using it, I felt better again. I then realised that she’d been wrong. This realisation resulted in me feeling frustrated with my doctor for not having listened. It also made me feel trapped – I wanted to feel safe and carry on using contraception, but I was unwilling to risk the debilitating side effects. I’ve heard this echoed time and time again in other people’s experiences.

My frustration led me to spend a lot (and I mean, a lot) of time reading and thinking about all of this. I began to wonder: how was my doctor so wrong? Why is it that, when so many of us are going through similar experiences, we are still unnoticed, dismissed as ‘hysterical’ or even ignored? Why do our bodies react so differently even when on exactly the same contraception? Has anyone done any research on any of this? There didn’t seem to be much. Why not?

As a result of all these unanswered questions, I felt there was a need for more conversation. And so, Control Freaks was created: a space where you can make your story heard, and find comfort in knowing that you are not alone in your experience.

This isn’t a space intended to spread anxiety about contraception, or to provide all the answers. Instead, this is a space to celebrate contraception when it works for us, and somewhere to talk about it when it doesn’t. It’s important to share all of our contraceptive stories, no matter our gender identities, ethnicities or backgrounds, and to create a conversation that is unstigmatized, informative and positive.

In the past, contraception increased the freedoms and choices available to people, and it should continue to do so in new and improved ways. My goal is to raise awareness of the often-overlooked nature of our contraceptive experiences. Control Freaks will exist as a persistent and determined reminder that in a world that is caught up in releasing new smartphones every week, everyday technologies like contraception also desperately need an update. There need to be improvements in the technology, the accessibility, the surrounding education and information, and in the medical care. All of this would contribute to the contraception that we are entitled to: safe, accessible and empowering.

So why Control Freaks? We’re reappropriating the term. Because there’s nothing wrong with wanting to be in control of our own bodies.

— Rea Caballero
